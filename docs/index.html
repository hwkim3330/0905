Implementation and Performance Evaluation of a 4-Port Credit-Based Shaper TSN Switch

QoS Provisioning in Automotive Ethernet

Research on Time-Sensitive Networking for Vehicle Networks

Abstract

With the recent evolution of electrical/electronic (E/E) architecture toward a zonal structure, in-vehicle networks are required to reliably transmit diverse traffic types, including high-bandwidth multimedia streams and critical control data. To meet this demand, we implemented a system using a Credit-Based Shaper (CBS) and conducted tests and performance evaluations using a 4-port automotive TSN (Time-Sensitive Networking) switch.

Our experimental setup included multiple video stream sources, two video receivers, and a best-effort (BE) traffic generator to induce network congestion. The results showed that applying CBS achieved stable playback and minimal frame loss through the priority control of time-sensitive video streams, even under network overload.

1. Introduction

1.1 Background

The electrical/electronic (E/E) architecture of modern vehicles is rapidly evolving from domain-centric to zone-based structures. This transformation is driven by the explosive growth in in-vehicle data traffic accompanying the development of autonomous driving, advanced driver assistance systems (ADAS), and infotainment systems.

1.2 TSN and Credit-Based Shaper

TSN is a set of standards developed by the IEEE 802.1 working group that defines various mechanisms to ensure real-time performance and reliability in standard Ethernet networks. The Credit-Based Shaper (CBS) defined in IEEE 802.1Qav is a traffic shaping mechanism that reserves bandwidth for each traffic class and controls bursts to make latency predictable.

2. Credit-Based Shaper Theory

2.1 CBS Operating Principle

CBS manages a credit value in real-time for each traffic class to determine transmission eligibility. Credits increase at the idleSlope rate when the queue is empty and decrease at the sendSlope rate during frame transmission.

When queue is empty:

credit_c(t) = credit_c(t-1) + idleSlope × Δt

When transmitting frames:

credit_c(t) = credit_c(t-1) + sendSlope × Δt

2.2 CBS Parameters

  • idleSlope: Rate of credit increase when queue is empty (guaranteed bandwidth)
  • sendSlope: Rate of credit decrease during transmission
  • hiCredit: Maximum credit limit
  • loCredit: Minimum credit limit

5. Conclusion

5.1 Key Findings

  • CBS successfully controls output rate according to idle-slope configuration (100 Mbps)
  • Port 8 showed proper CBS operation with controlled transmission
  • Output port maintains stable transmission rate under overload conditions
  • Further configuration refinement needed for Ports 10 and 11

5.2 Future Work

  • Ensure uniform CBS configuration across all input ports
  • Test various idle-slope parameters for optimization
  • Integrate Time-Aware Shaper (TAS) with CBS
  • Apply to real automotive multimedia traffic patterns

5.3 Impact

This research experimentally demonstrated the practical applicability of TSN mechanisms for QoS provisioning in automotive networks, suggesting that CBS-based traffic control can play an important role in future autonomous driving and connected car network architecture design.
